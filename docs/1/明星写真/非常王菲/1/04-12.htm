Faye Wong, Jeff Chang

- the divine duo of Chinese pop....heavenly voices....


This article was extracted from 8 Days Issue 313 (5th Oct - 12 Oct 1996), when Faye and Jeff both won the Most Popular Singers in the Singapore Hit Awards 1996. Only the sections on Faye will be published here.

Faye's Value

Superstar recluse? Iconoclast? Will the real Faye Wong please stand up? In this rare heart-to-heart conducted recently over Taiwan radio, we find out what makes the Chinese pop queen tick. Text: Louis Sung

Q: You started singing professionally in 1989, and released Coming Home in 1992.
Faye: Yes. 1992.

Q: I've always been curious about how totally changed you were when you returned from New York.
Faye: It's more accurate to say that I had changed back to the real me. From 1989 to 1991, I felt very lost. I wasn't myself at all. When I returned in 1992, people perceived a drastic change, but that was actually the true Faye Won. Before that, being new to Hongkong, I just let my record company make most of the decisions. I didn't know what was happening and thus lacked a lot of self-confidence.

Q: What happened in New York?
Faye: New york was another cultural shock as it was different from bothe Beijing (where Faye was born and bred) and Hongkong. But this time it shocked me into consciousness. You must have heard of real-life cases where someone loses his memory in the first accident, then regains it in another. It's a similar senario for me: a double cultural shock. It was about time I woke up and became myself again.

Q: Have you decided to move back to Beijing now and use Hongkong as a base for promotion?
Faye: I'll be spending most of my time in Beijing as most of my music collaborators are there and it's difficult for them to travel to Hongkong.

Q: What's your daily routine like? Is it anything like what you did in Chuingking Express (which marked Faye's acting debut)?
Faye: Of course not! I've never done so much housework is real life! The director Wong Kar Wai, wanted me to mop the floor, wipe the table, perform all kinds of cleaning jobs which I usually wouldn't do. Besides I'm not as neurotic as the character in the film.

Q: Let's talk about your latest album, Exaperation. This album - where you wrote all but 2 songs - seems to marl the start of 'Faye Wong's Creative Era'. So we can say goodbye to the old Faye Wong! Looking back now, how do you feel about your early songs?
Faye: It wasn't that bad then. But the way I'm now, my mental state and mood being totally different, I feel I'm artistically much better now.

Q: You used to cover alternative songs. And now you're writing them.
Faye: I've always wanted to do something like this. But the timing wasn't right until now.

Q: Your lyrics seem to give the impression that you are a despondent person by nature.
Faye: Taken at face value, my lyrics might seem pessimistic with song titles like 'Degenerate' and 'Doomsday'. These are parts of me like that, a 'greyish' kind of personality. What I wanted to do was to express a kind of inner struggle. Sometimes I see something in myself which I don't like, so I write about it hoping that this way, I can change it. But it's not so easy. Human beings have a lot of weaknesses which are hard to overcome. And it's also mixed with a sense of helplessness.

Q: Your songs in Exaperation make the listener feel like that's a good state of mind to be in...
Faye: ...It's not a state I want to be in, but it's something I can't run away from. I feel that being exaperated is a human emotion, so it's neither good nor bad. Mentally, we all have our ups and downs. When things become too mundane, we start getting grouchy...

Q: I especially like your last line: "Everything is fine; the only lacking is trouble."
Faye: I actually meant the opposite. When your life is uneventful, you begin to yearn for trouble.

Q: How do you create your music? Do you jam with the band?
Faye: We usually start with a music 'base', then follow that by writing the melody. In most cases, the lyrics come last. There's only one song in the current album where I wrote the lyrics first.

Q: Which was that?
Faye: 'Doomsday'. I wrote it one day while lying in bed in Hongkong.

Q: A line from the song says, "Desire buys everyting." That sounds very 'Hongkong' but why the tittle?
Faye: I named the song 'Doomsday' because I wanted to write about the uglier side of life.

Q: Is the world 'ugly' to you?
Faye: Perhaps I'm more sensitive to the less pleasant things in life. The more material desires you have, the more spiritually empty you feel. Which leads some to look for instant gratification. I'm quite pessimistic about things.

Q: So what can alleviate the imperfection?
Faye: Well, life is not all suffering. If it were, then you might as well not go on living! In between the suffering, there will be some wonderful things which attract you and give you a sense of hope. But then later it becomes...

...disappointment.
Faye: Yes, nothing is certain. And that's life. There's no way you can change it. So the only thing you can do is have some fun in the midst of suffering.

Fun in the midst of suffering!
Faye: Whenever there's pleasure, there's bound to be pain.

Q: So is singing pleasurable?
Faye: Half the time, I'd say.

Q: Then again, you're no longer the 'Shirley Wong' [Faye's original moniker when she entered showbiz] of 1989. You're the 'Faye Wong' of 1996, someone who's got more say over what you do.
Faye: Yes, things are much better now. But one still worries about the future. Like, what's my next album going to be like? Will I still have the inspiration?

Q: Your recording contract is also a subject which arouses a lot of interest...
Faye: (Deep sigh) Actually, a lot of this news is unfounded.

Q: That's because we're all curious about you. Even when you're 'hiding' in Beijing, the tabloids still manage to track you down.
Faye: (Another sigh) What can I do? I'm already trying to keep as low profile as possible. I can understand the pressure [on the tabloid reporters] to unearth scoops. Granted, they have their press freedom, but as human being, I too have my rights. If the media want me to respect them, then they should respect me instead of printing hurtful reports. I know ultimately it's entertainment news, with the emphasis on 'entertainment'. But they shouldn't go too far.

You should take it easier.
Faye: I'm already taking it very easy. I just hope everyone will give me a break.
