中文

CBETA Chinese Electronic Tripitaka    (Taisho Edition)

The "Chinese Buddhist Electronic Text Association" (CBETA) has been formally established on Feb. 15, 1998 with support from the Yinshun Foundation of North-America (http://www.yinshun.org), Puti Educational Foundation and Chung-Hwa Institute of Buddhist Studies (http://www.chibs.edu.tw) to create, maintain and distribute free of charge an electronic version of the Chinese Buddhist Triptitaka.

The CBETA Chinese Electronic Tripitaka is based on the Taisho Tripitaka 「大正新大藏」(© Daizo Shuppansha, Inc. 大藏出版株式社) Vols 1-55 and 85. The right to input and distribute this database has been officially granted by the copyright holder Daizo Shuppansha. We would like to express our gratitude for this.

Please refer to this introduction for more detailed information. Here is a list of all the people that contributed to the creation of this text database.

In addition to the scriptures in electronic form, we also include reference works that can be used with the texts. Prof. Charles A. Muller (Toyo Gakuen University) kindly gave permission to include his Dictionary of East Asian Buddhist Terms. Since this dictionary is being continually enhanced, we suggest users visit his homepage for updates.

The electronic version of the "Dictionary for Buddhist Studies" 佛W大o典 by Ding Fubao included with this CD-ROM was contributed by the Electronic Buddhadharma Society(EBS http://www.baus.org/ebs/index_c.html). We like to express our gratitude for this valuable contribution.

    This version has been created for the Microsoft HTMLHelp browser, which is included with Windows 98 (and available as update for Windows 95). The text has been normalized in the same way as the above versions, but the corrections to the Taisho text are now displayed in red. Every section of the Taisho page (eg. p0001, section a) displays on a HTML page by itself. For ease of reading and for search across section boundaries, the last two lines of every page have been repeated on the following page, with the repeated lines in this style.

Some of the bitmapped images used for display of rare characters are derived from a font from the Konjaku Mojikyo 今昔文字R project (http://www.mojikyo.gr.jp). Many thanks for making this font available.
